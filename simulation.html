What is Gel Electrophoresis?

  
Gel electrophoresis is a fundamental laboratory technique used to separate DNA, RNA, or protein molecules based on their size and electrical charge. When an electric field is applied across a gel matrix — typically agarose for nucleic acids or polyacrylamide for proteins — the negatively charged molecules migrate through the gel toward the positive electrode (anode). Smaller fragments travel faster and move farther through the porous gel matrix, while larger fragments are impeded and remain closer to the wells where the sample was loaded.


  
This technique is indispensable in modern molecular biology, genetics, forensic science, and clinical diagnostics. It enables scientists to visualize, compare, and analyze nucleic acid and protein samples with high resolution. The separated fragments appear as distinct bands that can be stained with fluorescent dyes such as ethidium bromide or SYBR Green and visualized under UV light.

    The above relationship, derived from the Ferguson equation, states that the logarithm of the molecular weight (size) of a DNA fragment is linearly proportional to its migration distance through the gel. This is the basis for using a DNA ladder — a set of fragments of known sizes — to estimate the size of unknown fragments.
  


  
Principle of Gel Electrophoresis

  
The principle of gel electrophoresis is based on two fundamental properties of molecules: their net electrical charge and their size. DNA and RNA molecules carry a uniform negative charge due to the phosphate groups in their backbone. When placed in an electric field, all nucleic acid molecules migrate in the same direction — toward the positive electrode — at a rate that depends primarily on their size and the gel concentration.


  
The gel acts as a molecular sieve. It is composed of a porous polymer network through which molecules must navigate. Smaller molecules pass through the pores with ease and move quickly, while larger molecules are physically obstructed and migrate more slowly. The result is a separation of molecules by size: a mixture of fragments loaded into a single well will spread out into discrete bands, each corresponding to a different size class.


  
Components of Gel Electrophoresis

  

    

      
    
        
  • Agarose Gel: A porous matrix derived from seaweed polysaccharide. Gel concentration (typically 0.8%–2%) determines the pore size and the range of fragment sizes that can be resolved. Higher concentrations separate smaller fragments more effectively.
    • 
        
  • Electrophoresis Tank: A buffer-filled horizontal chamber that holds the gel. Electrodes at each end connect to the power supply and establish the electric field across the gel.
    • 
        
  • Buffer Solution (TAE or TBE): Maintains a stable pH, conducts electricity through the gel, and prevents DNA degradation. TAE (Tris-Acetate-EDTA) and TBE (Tris-Borate-EDTA) are the most commonly used buffers in nucleic acid electrophoresis.
    • 
        
  • Power Supply: Provides constant DC voltage (50–150 V for DNA gels). Higher voltage increases migration speed but can cause band distortion and heat buildup.
    • 
      

    

    

      
    
        
  • DNA Ladder (Marker): A mixture of DNA fragments of precisely known sizes, loaded alongside experimental samples. It provides a size reference against which unknown fragment sizes are estimated by comparing migration distances.
    • 
        
  • Loading Dye: Mixed with samples before loading into wells. Contains a dense reagent (glycerol or sucrose) to make the sample sink into the well, and one or more tracking dyes (bromophenol blue, xylene cyanol) to allow visual monitoring of electrophoresis progress.
    • 
        
  • Ethidium Bromide / SYBR Green: Fluorescent intercalating dyes that bind to DNA. Under UV illumination, DNA-bound dye fluoresces brightly, revealing the position of bands within the gel.
    • 
        
  • UV Transilluminator: Emits ultraviolet light (typically 302 nm or 365 nm) to excite the fluorescent dye, making the DNA bands visible for photography and analysis.
    • 
      

    

  


  
Applications of Gel Electrophoresis

  
Gel electrophoresis has an extraordinarily broad range of applications across biological and biomedical sciences. In molecular cloning, it is used to verify the sizes of PCR products and restriction digestion fragments. In forensic science and DNA fingerprinting, electrophoretic patterns of short tandem repeats (STRs) are used to identify individuals with near-certainty. In clinical diagnostics, it is used to detect genetic mutations, diagnose hereditary diseases, and identify pathogens.


  
In genomics and sequencing, gel electrophoresis has historically been used to read DNA sequences (Sanger sequencing). In proteomics, SDS-PAGE (sodium dodecyl sulphate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis) separates proteins by molecular weight and is the basis for Western blotting. In RNA biology, gel electrophoresis is used to assess RNA integrity, verify transcript sizes, and analyze gene expression by Northern blotting. In environmental science, it is applied in microbial community profiling through techniques such as DGGE (denaturing gradient gel electrophoresis).


  

    Key Takeaway: Gel electrophoresis is one of the most universally applied techniques in the life sciences. Its power lies in its simplicity — by exploiting the predictable relationship between molecular size and migration distance in a porous matrix under an electric field, it allows researchers to separate, visualize, and quantify nucleic acids and proteins from complex biological mixtures with precision and reproducibility.
